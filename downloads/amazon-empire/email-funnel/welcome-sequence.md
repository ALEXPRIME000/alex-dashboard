### 🔒 EXCLUSIVE: *The Night She Came Back* — [Hero Name]'s POV

*[Town Name] — Early October*

The bell above the door hadn't rung in fourteen months.

[Hero Name] knew that because he'd counted. Not obsessively — at least, that's what he told himself in the quieter moments when honesty was harder to dodge. He'd counted because the silence had shape. Weight. The particular texture of something missing.

He was elbow-deep in flour when it happened.

The bell rang.

He didn't look up right away. Tuesday afternoons brought Mrs. Calloway for her sourdough and old Gerald Tate for whatever he could find to complain about. Neither of them made [Hero Name]'s hands go still in the dough.

But his hands went still.

He knew before he turned around. Some animal instinct — or maybe just the particular quality of the silence that followed the bell, like the whole room held its breath along with him.

*Don't let it be her. Don't let it be her.*

He turned around.

It was her.

[Heroine Name] looked the same and completely different, the way places do when you've been away long enough. Same dark hair catching the afternoon light through the window. Same way she stood with her weight slightly on her left foot, like she was always half-ready to leave. But there was something in her face — something cautious and tired and *there* — that hadn't existed in the girl who'd driven out of [Town Name] four years ago without looking back.

He had approximately three seconds before she spotted him.

He used one of them to breathe.

He used another to remind himself that whatever he felt in this moment was his problem, not hers, and he would keep it that way.

He used the third to put on the steadiest face he owned.

"[Heroine Name]."

Her eyes found him. And for one fractured second — one single, traitorous second — something crossed her face that looked almost like relief.

Then it was gone.

"[Hero Name]." Her voice was steady. Of course it was. She'd always been steadier than him when it counted. "I didn't know you worked here."

*I own it now*, he almost said. *I bought it after your grandmother died because I couldn't stand the idea of someone else touching her kitchen.*

What he said instead: "Just helping out."

Some lies are acts of self-preservation.

She nodded. Looked around the bakery with those careful eyes, cataloguing every change and every sameness. He watched her notice the new counter, the repainted walls, the same ancient oven her grandmother had cursed at every single morning for thirty years.

"It looks good," she said softly. Like it cost her something.

"We try," he said.

And because he was [Hero Name] and not anyone smarter, he asked: "Can I get you something?"

She almost smiled. Didn't quite make it.

"Just looking," she said.

He turned back to his dough.

*Yeah*, he thought. *Me too.*
