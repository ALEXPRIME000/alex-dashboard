### 1.2 BSR Sweet Spot Analysis

**BSR = Best Seller Rank on Amazon. Lower = more sales.**

| BSR Range | Estimated Daily Sales | Monthly Revenue (at $3.99) |
|-----------|----------------------|---------------------------|
| #1 – #100 | 100–1,000+ | $12,000–$120,000+ |
| #101 – #1,000 | 30–100 | $3,600–$12,000 |
| #1,001 – #5,000 | 10–30 | $1,200–$3,600 |
| **#5,001 – #20,000** | **3–10** | **$360–$1,200** ← **SWEET SPOT** |
| #20,001 – #100,000 | 1–3 | $120–$360 |
| #100,001+ | < 1/day | < $120 |

**The Sweet Spot for Entry:** Target a subcategory where the #20–#50 books have a BSR between **#5,000 – #20,000**. This means:
- There's demand (real sales happening)
- Competition is beatable (not saturated by major publishers)
- A new release can crack the top 20 within 30 days with a proper launch

**Red Flags to Avoid:**
- Category where top 20 all have BSR < #1,000 (dominated by established authors)
- Category where top 20 all have BSR > #50,000 (too little demand)

### 1.5 Kindle Unlimited (KU) vs. Direct Purchase Strategy

**Kindle Unlimited (KDP Select):**
- Book enrolled exclusively with Amazon for 90 days
- Readers pay ~$11.99/month for unlimited reading
- You earn per-page-read (KENP rate ≈ $0.004–0.005 per page)
- For a 30K word book (~130 pages): ~$0.52–0.65 per full read-through
- **PROS:** Romance readers LOVE KU. KU traffic from Amazon algorithms is significant.
- **CONS:** Exclusivity means no other platforms (no Kobo, Apple Books, etc.)

**Wide Distribution (Not in KDP Select):**
- List on Amazon + Draft2Digital → Apple Books, Kobo, Barnes & Noble, Google Play
- Earn 70% royalty on each sale everywhere
- **PROS:** Diversified income, potential for bigger sales on Apple Books (strong romance market)
- **CONS:** Much harder to launch, no KU page reads, algorithm support lower on Amazon

**Recommendation for New Authors in 2026:**
> **Start with KDP Select / Kindle Unlimited** for the first 90-day window. Romance readers are overwhelmingly KU subscribers. Establish rank and reviews, then evaluate going wide.

### 2.3 Story Structure: 3-Act Structure for 30K Words

**Word Count Distribution:**

```
ACT 1 — Setup (25% = ~7,500 words)
├── Opening Hook (Chapter 1–2): ~3,000 words
├── Inciting Incident (Chapter 3): ~1,500 words
└── Setup/World Building (Chapter 4–5): ~3,000 words

ACT 2A — Rising Action (25% = ~7,500 words)
├── First Meeting/Conflict Escalation (Chapter 6–8): ~4,500 words
└── Midpoint Shift / Moment of Connection (Chapter 9–10): ~3,000 words

ACT 2B — Complications (25% = ~7,500 words)
├── Deepening Relationship (Chapter 11–13): ~4,500 words
└── Dark Moment / Black Moment setup (Chapter 14–15): ~3,000 words

ACT 3 — Resolution (25% = ~7,500 words)
├── Black Moment / Crisis (Chapter 16–17): ~3,000 words
├── Climax / Breakthrough (Chapter 18–19): ~3,000 words
└── Resolution / HEA (Chapter 20): ~1,500 words
```

**Key Romance Beats (Required for Genre):**
1. Meet-cute or explosive first scene together
2. First moment of chemistry / attraction
3. First moment of real vulnerability (one character opens up)
4. First kiss / physical escalation (heat level dependent)
5. Midpoint connection deepens the bond
6. External conflict threatens relationship
7. Internal fear revealed (the thing that could keep them apart)
8. Black moment — they're torn apart (misunderstanding, secret revealed, choice made)
9. Grovel / grand gesture — one character fights for the other
10. HEA (Happily Ever After) or HFN (Happy For Now)

### 2.6 Conflict Planning

**Internal Conflict (the emotional core — what keeps them apart from the inside):**
- Heroine: Fear of abandonment / being left again / not being enough
- Hero: Fear of letting people in / believing he'll hurt her / past relationship damage

**External Conflict (the plot — what keeps them apart from the outside):**
- She's only in town temporarily
- He owns something / controls something she needs
- A community conflict puts them on opposite sides
- A secret that, if revealed, changes everything

**The Conflict Intersection:**
> The best romances have internal and external conflicts that MIRROR each other.  
> Example: Heroine fears being abandoned (internal) + she's only here temporarily and might leave (external) → both characters are afraid of exactly the same thing from opposite directions.

### 2.8 Writing a Compelling Hook (Chapter 1)

**The First Chapter Must Do 5 Things:**
1. Establish the heroine's voice immediately
2. Show her in motion (don't start with backstory)
3. Plant the central tension/question
4. Hint at the wound/flaw without explaining it
5. Get the hero into the story by the end of chapter 1 (or early chapter 2)

**Hook Formulas:**
```
Option A — In-Scene Action Hook:
"[Character] is doing [action] when [inciting moment] happens."
Example: "I had exactly forty-seven dollars and a dead cellphone when I drove into Cedarville — which is exactly forty-seven dollars more than I deserved after blowing up my entire life."

Option B — Bold Statement Hook:
Start with a confession, secret, or bold claim.
Example: "The last person I ever expected to see in the emergency vet clinic at 2 AM was Cole Mercer — but there he was, cradling a three-legged cat and looking murderous about it."

Option C — Conflict-First Hook:
Open in the middle of conflict.
Example: "The sign on the door said PRIVATE PROPERTY, KEEP OUT. I chose to interpret that as a suggestion."
```

**First Page Checklist:**
- [ ] Voice is distinctive within the first paragraph
- [ ] Reader knows who the heroine is
- [ ] At least one sensory detail (smell, sound, touch)
- [ ] A hint of the internal wound (not explained — just hinted)
- [ ] A question or tension that makes the reader turn the page

### 2.9 Writing a Satisfying Ending That Teases the Series

**The HEA Chapter Must Deliver:**
1. Full emotional resolution — both characters' wounds addressed
2. The declaration / acknowledgment (explicit "I love you" or equivalent)
3. A vision of their future together
4. A callback to a symbol or moment from chapter 1 (full circle)

**The Series Tease:**
- Introduce or re-feature a supporting character who's clearly got a story
- Leave a dangling thread that hints at Book 2's conflict
- Add an epilogue or "sneak peek" with the first chapter of Book 2

**Ending Structure:**
```
Resolution Scene (Chapter 19): Reconciliation — they choose each other
HEA Scene (Chapter 20, Part 1): Declaration + commitment
Epilogue (Chapter 20, Part 2): 3-6 months later — happy life glimpse + series setup
Bonus Teaser: "Coming Next in Cedarville Hearts..." + Chapter 1 of Book 2
```

## SECTION 3: WRITING THE BOOK

### 3.1 AI-Assisted Writing Workflow

**The Master Workflow:**
```
OUTLINE → SCENE BRIEFS → AI DRAFT → HUMAN POLISH → FINAL DRAFT
```

**Step-by-Step AI Writing Process:**

**Phase 1: Prep (1-2 days)**
- Complete character profiles (Section 2.5)
- Complete 20-chapter outline (Section 2.4)
- Write 2-sentence scene brief for every scene (~40 scenes total)
- Establish style guide (Section 3.4)

**Phase 2: First Draft by AI (5-10 days)**
For each chapter, use this prompt structure:
```
PROMPT TEMPLATE:
"You are writing Chapter [X] of a contemporary small town romance novel.

STYLE GUIDE:
- POV: [First person / Third person close]
- Voice: [Heroine's voice description]
- Heat level: [Sweet / Steamy / Explicit]

- Previous chapter ended with: [1-2 sentences]

**Phase 4: Full Draft Assembly**
- Compile all chapters
- Read in full for continuity
- Fill gaps and inconsistencies
- Send to editing

### 3.2 Quality Standards

**Voice Consistency:**
- Keep a "voice bible" — 10-15 example lines that capture your narrator's personality
- Reference it when prompting AI for new chapters
- Watch for: formal vs. casual language shifts, vocabulary drift, humor disappearing

**Pacing:**
- Short chapters = faster pace (romance readers prefer 1,500-2,500 word chapters)
- End each chapter on a mini-cliffhanger or emotional hook
- Alternate high-tension scenes with quieter connection scenes

**Emotional Beats:**
- Every chapter should hit at least one emotional note (longing, frustration, hope, fear, joy)
- Reader should feel something at the end of every chapter
- Use the "reader's stomach" test: does this scene create butterflies, heartbreak, or giddy excitement?

### 3.3 POV Decisions

**First Person vs. Third Person:**

| | First Person | Third Person Close |
|---|---|---|
| Feel | Intimate, immediate | Slightly more distance |
| Flexibility | One POV at a time | Easier to switch POVs |
| Market | Very popular in contemporary romance | Standard for all subgenres |
| AI ease | Slightly harder to maintain | Easier for AI consistency |

**Recommendation:** First person dual POV (heroine + hero alternating chapters) — most popular in contemporary romance 2024–2026.

**Dual POV Rules:**
- Label each chapter with the POV character's name
- Hero's POV chapters are essential — they let readers see his feelings before the heroine does
- Hero should think about her differently than she thinks about herself (he sees her beauty, strength, she sees her flaws)
- Avoid "head hopping" — one POV per chapter or scene break

### 3.4 Dialogue Best Practices for Romance

**The Rules of Romance Dialogue:**
1. **Every line must do double duty** — advance plot OR reveal character (ideally both)
2. **Subtext is queen** — what they DON'T say is as important as what they say
3. **Banter is currency** — witty back-and-forth = sexual/romantic tension without physical touch
4. **Internal reaction follows dialogue** — character's internal response to what was said
5. **Dialogue beats > dialogue tags** — "he said" is invisible; "he set down his coffee" is cinematic

**Bad vs. Good Dialogue:**
```
BAD: "I don't like you," she said angrily.
"I don't care," he replied coldly.

GOOD: "I don't like you." She said it like a warning.
He looked at her for a long moment. "Yeah." A half-smile that didn't reach his eyes.
"You already made that clear."
```

**Heat-Level Dialogue Calibration:**
- Sweet/Clean: tension through almost-touches and loaded words
- Steamy: more explicit acknowledgment of attraction, double meanings
- Explicit: direct, physical, uninhibited (check market standards first)

## SECTION 4: EDITING & QUALITY CONTROL

### 4.1 Self-Editing Checklist (Plot Holes, Pacing, Character Consistency)

**Run this on the complete first draft:**

**Plot & Logic:**
```
□ Every chapter serves a purpose (cut anything that doesn't)
□ No plot holes (check: character motivations, timeline, geography)
□ Every conflict set up has a payoff
□ The black moment is earned (not random or contrived)
□ The resolution is earned (not too easy)
□ All subplots are tied up or intentionally left for next book
□ Timeline is consistent (track day-by-day if needed)
```

**Character Consistency:**
```
□ Heroine behaves consistently with her established personality
□ Hero's change is gradual and believable
□ Supporting characters don't disappear and reappear randomly
□ Character motivations make sense throughout
□ No character suddenly knows something they shouldn't
```

**Pacing:**
```
□ No chapter longer than 2,500 words (tight pacing)
□ No more than 2 "quiet" chapters in a row
□ The midpoint actually shifts the story
□ The black moment hits hardest (emotionally)
□ Tension escalates from chapter 1 to chapter 20
```

---

### 4.2 Line Editing Pass

**What to look for:**

**Grammar & Mechanics:**
- Comma splices, run-on sentences
- Inconsistent verb tense
- Pronoun antecedent confusion
- Dialogue punctuation errors

**Flow & Rhythm:**
- Vary sentence length (short/long/medium mix)
- Cut adverbs (replace with stronger verbs)
- Eliminate "was -ing" constructions where possible
- Watch for passive voice in action scenes

**Word Choice:**
- Kill filler words: very, really, just, that, quite, almost, seemed
- Replace weak verbs: walked → strode, said → muttered/snapped/whispered
- Cut redundant phrases: "she thought to herself", "he nodded his head"
- Check for repeated words in close proximity

**Romance-Specific:**
- Don't over-use character names in dialogue (sounds unnatural)
- Vary internal monologue tags (don't always use "I thought")
- Ensure physical descriptions don't become clinical during intimate scenes

### 4.3 Proofreading Pass

**After line editing, run a final proofread:**
1. Change the font or background color (tricks your brain into seeing fresh)
2. Read aloud (catches rhythm problems and typos your eye skips)
3. Use text-to-speech tools to listen to the full book
4. Check every proper noun for consistency (character names, place names)
5. Verify chapter numbers and headers are correct

- Bold, punchy font

- Fantasy elements (mist, forest, supernatural lighting)

- Rich, saturated colors

### 5.6 Paperback Cover Template

**Spine Width Calculation:**
```
Spine Width (inches) = Page Count × Paper Thickness
White paper: Page count × 0.0025 inches
Cream paper: Page count × 0.0025 inches (KDP cream is same)

For a 200-page book: 200 × 0.0025 = 0.5 inches spine
```

**Full Cover Template (5x8 trim size):**
```
Total Width = Back Cover (5") + Spine + Front Cover (5") + Bleed (0.125" each side)
Total Height = 8" + Bleed (0.125" top and bottom)

Example for 200-page book:
Total Width = 5 + 0.5 + 5 + 0.25 = 10.75 inches
Total Height = 8 + 0.25 = 8.25 inches
At 300 DPI: 3,225 × 2,475 pixels
```

**KDP's Cover Calculator:** Use kdp.amazon.com/en_US/cover-calculator for exact specs.

### 7.8 Paperback Pricing

**Formula:**
```
Minimum Price = Printing Cost × 1.0 + Distribution Fee
Your Price = Printing Cost + Your Royalty + Distribution

KDP Printing Cost (B&W interior, 200 pages, 5x8):
US: ~$2.55 fixed + $0.015/page = $2.55 + $3.00 = $5.55 printing cost
Minimum list price to earn royalty: ~$7.99
Recommended price: $11.99–$13.99

Royalty = (List Price × 0.60) - Printing Cost
At $12.99: ($12.99 × 0.60) - $5.55 = $7.79 - $5.55 = $2.24/sale
```

### 7.9 KDP Select / Kindle Unlimited — Pros & Cons

**PROS of KDP Select:**
- Access to Kindle Unlimited library (your biggest traffic source)
- Eligible for Kindle Countdown Deals (timed discount promotions)

**Recommendation:**

## SECTION 9: SERIES STRATEGY

### 9.1 Why Series Outsell Standalones in Romance

**The Math:**
```
Standalone reader: 1 book × $3.99 = $3.99 per reader
Series reader: 4 books × $3.99 = $15.96 per reader

With a strong series:
- Book 1 at $0.99 (funnel)
- Books 2-4 at $3.99-4.99
- Box set (Books 1-3) at $7.99-9.99

One reader who loves your world = $20-30 lifetime value
```

**Reader Psychology:**
- Romance readers are hungry for MORE of characters they love
- A familiar world reduces purchase friction for Book 2+
- KU page reads amplify exponentially with each new book (algorithm boost)
- Series readers are the most loyal, highest-value segment

---

### 9.2 Shared World / Interconnected Standalones

**The Interconnected Standalone Approach (Recommended):**
- Each book features a NEW couple (different hero + heroine)
- But set in the SAME world/town/group of friends
- Each book stands alone (new reader can start at any book)
- BUT supporting characters from Book 1 appear in Book 2, etc.

**Why This Works:**
- Readers can start at any entry point (easier to find your work)
- Amazon can recommend any book in the series
- Readers who fall in love with a side character MUST read their book
- No cliff-hangers — satisfying HEA each time

**Cedarville Hearts Example:**
```
Book 1: The Trouble with Coming Home (Emma + Cole)
Book 2: Reckless Hearts (Sheriff Jake from Book 1 + new heroine)
Book 3: Wild at Home (Cole's sister + the rival rancher from Book 1)
Book 4: Second Bloom (The florist from Book 1 + the newcomer)
```

---

### 9.3 Planting Seeds for Future Books

**How to Set Up Books 2, 3, 4 in Book 1:**

1. **Introduce future heroes as complex, interesting side characters**
   - Give them a line or moment that makes readers wonder about them
   - Hint at their wound or backstory without revealing everything

2. **Create unresolved threads**
   - A local conflict that will escalate in Book 2
   - A mystery about a character that gets answered in Book 3
   - A tension between side characters that bubbles under the surface

3. **The End Tease**
   - The last chapter of Book 1 can include a glimpse of Book 2's couple
   - Or an epilogue that shows Book 2's inciting event

**Seed Planting Rules:**
- Don't sacrifice Book 1's story for setup (readers will notice)
- Seeds should feel organic, not forced
- Max 2-3 seeds per book (don't overwhelm)

---

### 9.4 Box Set Strategy

**When to Release:**
- After Book 3 is published
- Bundle Books 1-3 in a single ebook

**Pricing:**
```
Individual books: $3.99 each = $11.97 total
Box set: $7.99 (saves reader $3.98)
Royalty: $7.99 × 70% = $5.59/sale (vs. $2.79 per individual book)
```

**Box Set Benefits:**
- New category opportunities (collections/box sets category)
- Larger KENP page reads per KU read-through
- Higher perceived value
- Amazon promotes bundles to readers who bought Book 1

**Box Set Cover:**
- Combine all 3 covers in a triptych or new unified design
- "The Complete Cedarville Hearts Collection: Books 1-3"

---

### 9.5 Read-Through Rate Optimization

**Read-Through Rate:** The percentage of Book 1 readers who go on to buy Book 2.

**Industry benchmarks:**
- Average: 30-40% read-through from Book 1 to Book 2
- Good: 50-60%
- Excellent: 70%+

**How to Improve Read-Through:**
1. **Strong Book 1 ending** — HEA must be satisfying BUT leave them wanting more of the world
2. **Compelling preview** — include Chapter 1 of Book 2 at the end of Book 1
3. **Immediate availability** — release Book 2 within 6-12 weeks of Book 1
4. **Back matter CTA** — direct, enthusiastic link to Book 2
5. **Price strategy** — consider making Book 1 permanently $0.99 to increase Book 2+ sales volume

**Tracking Read-Through:**
```
Month 1 after Book 1 launch: 100 sales (Book 1)
Month 2 after Book 2 launch: 43 sales (Book 2)
Read-through rate: 43%
```

### 11.2 Trope Selection Rationale

**Selected Tropes:**
1. **Small Town Romance** — Setting and premise trope. Enormous, evergreen market. Strong subcategory on Amazon. Immediately signals "cozy, heartfelt, community-driven" to readers.

2. **Enemies to Lovers** — Relationship dynamic trope. #1 most popular romance trope globally (consistent Goodreads data). Creates natural tension without contrived misunderstandings. Readers emotionally invested from page 1.

3. **Forced Proximity** — Plot device trope. Escalates the enemies-to-lovers dynamic. Naturally confines characters together (job site, small space, shared work). Reader-loved because it accelerates the "they can't escape each other" energy.

**Why This Combination Works:**
- Small Town = setting that enables forced proximity (small community, shared spaces)
- Enemies to Lovers = built-in conflict engine (no need to manufacture tension)
- Forced Proximity = plot device that makes avoidance impossible
- Triple trope stack = three separate Amazon/TikTok search communities all discovering this book

**Comparable Titles (comp books):**
- *The Kiss Curse* by Erin Sterling (small town, forced proximity)
- *Happy Place* by Emily Henry (forced proximity, second chance, small town)

### 11.4 Complete 20-Chapter Outline

```
CHAPTER 1 — Emma Arrives (Emma POV)
Hook: Emma's car breaks down at the Cedarville town limit sign.
Scene 1: She's standing next to her dead car, staring at the "Welcome Back" sign, 
          cursing under her breath. She's here to sell the house, not stay.
Scene 2: Jake Mercer (not knowing who she is) stops to help. She doesn't recognize 
          him (he was 15 when she left). He calls his brother.
Beat: Hook. Voice established. Stakes: she wants to be in and out.

CHAPTER 2 — Wrong First Impression (Cole POV)
Hook: Cole drives out expecting an abandoned car. He doesn't expect Emma Cole.
Scene 1: Recognition. They haven't spoken in 5 years. Their last conversation ended badly (we don't know exactly how yet).
Scene 2: Cole tows her car to town. Minimal words. Maximum tension.
Beat: Hero introduced. First clash. Backstory hinted.

CHAPTER 3 — The House (Emma POV)
Hook: Emma sees the state of Nana Bea's house — it's worse than she thought.
Scene 1: She walks through, cataloguing what needs to be done. 
          A note from Nana Bea in the kitchen drawer. Emotional gut punch.
Scene 2: Dolly from the diner brings food, tells her who's been watching the house: Cole.
Beat: Inciting incident — she can't just list it. She needs to renovate it first.

CHAPTER 4 — Non-Negotiable (Both POV alternating)
Hook: Emma has to hire Cole to do the renovation. There is no other contractor.
Scene 1: Emma at Cole's office. She asks. He almost says no.
Scene 2: He says yes, but his terms are clear: professional, efficient, no personal history.
Beat: Forced Proximity established. Ground rules set (to be immediately broken).

CHAPTER 5 — Day One (Emma POV)
Hook: First day working together. It's awful. And weirdly not.
Scene 1: The crew arrives. Roy is warm. Cole is not. Emma is in the way.
Scene 2: Accidental moment — Cole catches Emma when she almost falls through a rotten floorboard.
          Half-second where neither of them moves.
Beat: First physical contact. First crack in armor.

CHAPTER 6 — Dolly's Diner (Cole POV)
Hook: Cole overhears something about Emma he wasn't supposed to hear.
Scene 1: Cole at the diner, hears Dolly talking to Priya — Emma lost her job.
          She's not going back to a career. She's going back to nothing.
Scene 2: Cole doesn't say anything about it. But he looks at her differently.
Beat: Hero gains new understanding. His protectiveness starts to activate.

CHAPTER 7 — Emma's Walls (Emma POV)
Hook: Priya arrives in Cedarville ("support visit" — she's nosy).
Scene 1: Emma tells Priya: "It's fine, I'm fine, Cole is fine, everything is fine."
Scene 2: Priya: "Your eye is twitching, Em." Emma: "It does that." Priya: "Only when you're lying."
Beat: Emma's internal wound exposed to the reader via confession to Priya.

CHAPTER 8 — A Mutual Disaster (Both POV)
Hook: A pipe bursts. Everything is wet. Someone needs to make decisions.
Scene 1: The emergency. They work together instinctively — Cole calls commands, Emma executes without question.
Scene 2: Aftermath. Soaked. Standing very close. Neither moving away.
Beat: Teamwork. The first glimpse of what they could be together.

CHAPTER 9 — Coffee at Midnight (Emma POV)
Hook: Emma can't sleep. Cole is still on the property, doing a post-flood check.
Scene 1: She finds him at the kitchen table with her grandmother's old coffee pot.
          They drink coffee. Almost in the dark. Actually talking for the first time.
Scene 2: He tells her about his father's heart attack. She tells him about Nana Bea's diagnosis.
          They're both people taking care of something that's slipping away.
Beat: Vulnerability. First real connection. Almost-touch that doesn't happen.

CHAPTER 10 — MIDPOINT: The Photograph (Cole POV)
Hook: Cole finds a photograph in the wall cavity during demolition: young Emma and young Cole, 
      at a town fair, laughing.
Scene 1: He gives it to her. She's undone by it. So is he.
Scene 2: They almost kiss. She pulls back. He lets her. But something has permanently shifted.
Beat: MIDPOINT. The relationship is real now. Both know it. Both are afraid.

CHAPTER 11 — The Town Sees (Emma POV)
Hook: Cedarville has noticed. Everyone is talking.
Scene 1: Dolly hints. Jake is insufferably delighted. Roy nods at Cole with meaning.
Scene 2: Emma tells herself: "It's not a thing." She makes dinner for the crew anyway.
Beat: Deepening bond. She's being absorbed into this life against her will and she loves it.

CHAPTER 12 — The Offer (Cole POV)
Hook: An external threat: a developer wants to buy Nana Bea's land if the house doesn't sell quickly.
Scene 1: Cole confronts Emma about the developer's offer that came through him (he's connected to the town).
Scene 2: Emma is furious he didn't tell her. He's furious she'd consider selling to a developer 
          who'd tear it down. It's the first real fight that matters.
Beat: External conflict. Stakes for the property raised. Their values revealed.

CHAPTER 13 — What She Left Behind (Emma POV)
Hook: Emma goes through Nana Bea's things. Finds letters.
Scene 1: Letters between Nana Bea and Cole from the past 5 years — Nana asked him to watch the house.
          He's been taking care of her grandmother's home while Emma was gone.
Scene 2: She confronts him about it. Quietly. Not angry this time.
          He: "She asked me not to tell you." Her: "Why did you say yes?" Him: [long pause] "Because she loved you."
Beat: The deepest revelation. He's been carrying something for her without being asked.

CHAPTER 14 — The First Time (Both POVs)
Hook: The tension breaks.
Scene 1: She kisses him first. He kisses her back. It goes further than either planned.
Scene 2: Aftermath — both in the warm light of the kitchen. This is real. This is terrifying.
Beat: First intimacy. The relationship is physical now. The fear is higher too.

CHAPTER 15 — The Almost-Perfect Week (Emma POV)
Hook: They have seven days of something that feels like a life.
Scene 1: Working together during the day. Together at night. Town starting to look at them like a unit.
Scene 2: Priya: "You look happy." Emma realizes she does. She's terrified of it.
Beat: The calm before the storm. Everything is beautiful. Which means it's about to break.

CHAPTER 16 — BLACK MOMENT: Amy Returns (Cole POV)
Hook: Cole's ex-fiancée Amy shows up in Cedarville with news: she's moving back.
Scene 1: Emma sees them talking outside. From a distance. Reads it wrong. 
          Cole doesn't see Emma see it.
Scene 2: Emma overhears a conversation in the diner — Amy and Dolly — Amy says she 
          regrets leaving Cole. Emma hears: "he never got over her." She doesn't wait for context.
Beat: BLACK MOMENT. Emma's wound activates. "I knew this would happen. Everyone leaves or 
       someone better comes back." She decides to leave first.

CHAPTER 17 — The Leaving (Both POVs)
Hook: Emma calls a real estate agent. Cole arrives at the house to find boxes.
Scene 1: She tells him she's listing the property and leaving by end of week.
          He doesn't fight it. (He's afraid of exactly this.) He says: "Okay."
Scene 2: Both perspectives: both are devastated. Both are also completely believing their worst 
          fear about themselves.
Beat: The lowest point. Both doing the wrong thing for understandable reasons.

CHAPTER 18 — GRAND GESTURE: The Thing Cole Builds (Cole POV)
Hook: Roy tells Cole what he saw — Emma's face when she saw him with Amy. 
      Roy: "She didn't leave because she doesn't care. She left because she does."
Scene 1: Cole goes to see Amy. They have the conversation they never had. She says: 
          "I came back for closure, not you. I have a whole life. Are you seriously still using me as an excuse?"
Scene 2: Cole drives to the city. He finds Emma packing boxes in a borrowed apartment.
          He doesn't have words. He hands her something: the photograph from the wall. 
          And the deed to the house with her name already on it — Nana Bea had already transferred it.
Beat: GRAND GESTURE. He's not asking her to stay. He's giving her the choice, free and clear, with no pressure.

CHAPTER 19 — Coming Home (Emma POV)
Hook: Emma with the deed and the photograph. She thinks about leaving and being left.
Scene 1: She calls Nana Bea's memory care facility. Sits with her for an hour.
          Nana doesn't always remember. But today she says: "Are you still running, baby girl?"
Scene 2: Emma drives back to Cedarville. She doesn't know what she'll find.
          Cole is on the porch. He's been there a while.
Beat: Reconciliation. The walls come fully down. She chooses. He receives it.

CHAPTER 20 — HEA + Series Tease (Both POVs)
Hook: Six months later.
Scene 1: Emma has taken the event planning remote, based in Cedarville. 
          Nana Bea's house is finished — and she's living in it.
Scene 2: Cole's brother Jake is at the hardware store when someone new walks in 
          (future heroine of Book 2 — brief, tantalizing glimpse).
Epilogue: Emma and Cole at the town fair — the same one in the photograph. Full circle.
Beat: HEA. Series seed planted. Reader wants Book 2.
